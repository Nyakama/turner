Brazil: ready, tech, go

Andre Rizzo

Associate Director

Latin America

Email +55 11 47667185
Andre Rizzo 02 March 2020

The impact of new technology is increasing the pace of change in Latin America’s largest economy. As companies grow and business models change, this presents real opportunities for the country’s new wave of contractors.

Brazil is South America’s largest country by both population and area and is the fifth largest country in the world. Over 209 million people live in Brazil, 85 percent of whom live in its cities, driving a need for increased housing, employment and investment.

After a challenging period of recession and turbulence, Brazil is re-emerging as an attractive proposition for a broad range of companies and investors.

The rise is slow and steady. There are hopes that the federal government’s pro-business agenda will lead to increased investor confidence. New multinational businesses are arriving, and more established ones are expanding their operations.

Take Uber, which came to Brazil in 2014; from a couple of dozen employees, it has made the country its second largest market – losing only to the US. Now with hundreds of employees, Uber has outgrown its headquarters in São Paulo three times in the last few years.

Uber is just one of many multinationals that has been drawn to Brazil in the past five-to-ten years. Other major tech companies which are now established and expanding, mostly in the country’s commercial heart, São Paulo, include Google, Amazon, and Microsoft.

São Paulo is also home to Brazil’s thriving start-up community, which has bred successes such as fintech company Nubank and gaming creator Wildlife, companies which are now expanding from the city into the global market.

Tech challenges

Everything happens fast in São Paulo. With technology-driven changes creating opposing problems for the country’s new start-ups and established companies, both groups are looking to expand their real estate portfolios to meet their evolving business needs.

Growing tech companies need to find property fast, but with no real estate professionals on their leadership team, it’s easy to make costly mistakes. They need flexible and lean real estate processes that grow with their business. Initially, the driver is to find space fast; later, they need to start considering opex alongside capex in their decision making.

Larger companies are keen to deploy new technology, such as enterprise resource planning (ERP) software, but systems that don’t work with their company culture and goals can add additional risk. Too often we see deployment of new technology for technology’s sake, with real estate departments trying to shoehorn their processes into solutions that don’t fit.

The aim should be to use technology as a tool to make real estate programmes more agile and efficient, while still meeting corporate governance constraints.

In other sectors, technological changes are creating new opportunities for the construction sector. Retailers are re-thinking their property portfolios, using less space for storage due to automated inventory controls. High-tech and manufacturing companies are planning investments in automation and upgrades to production lines to deliver new products.

With this healthy pipeline of construction activity in sight, and the first tier of Brazil’s contractors decimated by the corruption scandals that rocked the country, there might be concern over the capacity of the industry to deliver. The good news is that there is a new wave of tier-two contractors, gearing up to take on larger projects. In a new decade, and with a clean slate, this new order has the opportunity to do things differently.
