South Africa's private sector: opportunities in the Mozambique gas programme

27 February 2020

It’s been a decade in the making, but the time is now for South African businesses, which are facing low demand for products at home, to explore the Mozambique gas market.

The development of three catalytic, multibillion-dollar gas projects in Mozambique presents significant opportunities to supply both the project value chain, as well as the ancillary projects that will see Palma transform from a small fishing village to the size of Aberdeen over the next few years.

These opportunities are not without their difficulties. Seizing them will require significant coordination between the public and private sectors, in both South Africa and Mozambique, for businesses to compete effectively in bidding processes. But the rewards are well worth the effort.

Natural gas in Mozambique - a sizeable opportunity

Mozambique is set to become one of the world’s leading liquefied natural gas (LNG) exporters and a major energy player globally. Several key projects are under way and, once completed, will create a number of additional projects in domestic gas development. For more information on the specific projects please read our article ‘Momentum grows in Mozambique mega gas projects, but multiple challenges lie ahead’.

This is the biggest undertaking of its kind in Africa – and it’s right on our doorstep. The Mozambique developments are considerably bigger than the Gorgon natural gas project in Western Australia – one of the world's largest LNG projects and the largest single-resource project in Australia to date – in terms of both financial investment decision (FID) and output.

Onshore construction in Mozambique could require over 20,000 people working on competing projects at the same time.

In Area 1 alone, approximately USD0.5bn has been set aside for local content funding from South Africa and US$5 bn for local Mozambique content. Another US$5 bn is earmarked for local Mozambique content by 2023. For South African businesses registered and paying taxes in Mozambique, that’s US$11 bn worth of opportunities.

There are massive ancillary and downstream opportunities attached to these projects. The question for South African businesses is not, ‘should we get involved?’ but, ‘how can we afford not to?’

Gas projects in Mozambique - where the potential lies

The major engineering, procurement and construction (EPC) contracts for the LNG and FNLG projects have been awarded, however, there is still tremendous potential across the supply chain for businesses. Projects of this scale have never been built in Africa. With the limited skills available in Mozambique and the fact that Palma is a remote site, it means there are plenty of opportunities for South African companies who are prepared to invest and get involved in these projects.

The typical LNG supply chain has numerous requirements – giving a wealth of opportunities for South African businesses to subcontract in diverse areas.

In addition to the offshore and onshore services for LNG trains, reservoirs, wells, upstream facilities, marine and subsea, and the LNG itself, there’s a great deal of potential in related services (such as catering, facilities, accommodation, transport, logistics, financial and legal services, recruitment and training) and supporting industries – food and beverage, retail, clinics, schools and hotels.

Businesses looking for opportunities should cast their nets wide and look at the infrastructure and services needing to be created around these developments and not only the gas programme itself. This is where they can supply growing towns and new facilities. There is also the opportunity to work with local Mozambicans to build long-term sustainable businesses over the next 30 years of the project and into LNG operations.

Those that get in on the ground now, will be at an advantage as these surrounding areas develop.

Mozambique will be a construction site until 2030, so any undertaking will require a long-term perspective – as well as patience and careful partner selection – to see the benefits. 

Contracts are set up to transfer risk to service providers, so it’s critical to fully understand what is involved. Local content, upskilling and partnerships are a critical feature of everyday business decisions when operating across borders. You will need to understand and be able to meet those requirements before concluding any agreement.

It will also take a lot of commitment to handle some of the challenges of working in the region. Red tape, logistics issues and compliance with local-content requirements are no small matter. The site is remote, and infrastructure, skills and resources are limited. Living conditions are tough and security risks are a concern.

While some of these issues will subside with further development, you need to know what to expect both in the short term and for the long term.

Creating the path to procurement for the South African manufacturing industry

Don’t expect an invitation

Most South African providers are unknown to the large international organisations running the main contracts. You’ll therefore need to actively get in front of and introduce your organisation to the EPC organisations responsible for procuring sub-contractors. Some of these contacts are based in Houston, London and Milan.

While many work packages will be given to big international suppliers, they will all be looking for local capability to de-risk projects and to reduce costs.

The good news is these companies do have an interest in partnering with South African businesses. This is due to their proximity, local export capabilities, and its reputation of traditionally having a strong construction sector.

Use existing political and institutional frameworks

In February 2019, the Governments of South Africa and Mozambique signed a memorandum of understanding (MOU) on economic cooperation. This new agreement signals a ‘new deal’ with Mozambique – one that is complementary and mutually reinforcing.

The Department of Trade and Industry (the dti) is focusing on understanding Mozambique’s development strategies, building confidence in South Africa’s capabilities as a strategic partner, and identifying projects to cement the partnership. The department seeks to work closely with businesses to identify opportunities and collaborate to establish successful operations across the border.

Private sector initiatives, such as Standard Bank’s Mozambique Incubator, also offers a range of services to small business. These include access to market opportunities and finance, and assistance in partnering with local and international companies that plan to operate within the country.

Take a collective approach

The dti is eager to create a platform where industry bodies and individual corporations work together to implement a package deal that harnesses the collective financial and technical resources of “SA Inc”.

Having a framework that brings together diverse but complementary interests, an understanding of the relevant political mechanisms in play, and the ability to engage at the right level, in the right way, will help in securing opportunities across the value chain.

Seizing the opportunities for the South African manufacturing industry

In a recent Turner & Townsend survey of the South African manufacturing industry, the main concerns about seizing business opportunities relating to the gas programme in Mozambique included a lack of knowledge about the gas programme and projects, partnering with locals, logistics, registration and procurement to name a few. Based on our delivery of projects in Africa for the last 38 years (including over a decade in Mozambique), we have the following advice:

Tips to successfully navigate the opportunity

  • Be mindful of the visas required to work in Mozambique. A tourist visa will not allow you access to work opportunities.
  • Local capacity is an issue, but localisation is very important. There is a preferential procurement mechanism allowing 20 percent price uplift for local procurement on the gas programme. Therefore, you need to plan projects with an understanding of the existing supply chain and how you will partner locally and give back to the community (e.g. via job creation, skills transfer or training), so that the benefits are balanced and there’s a win-win for all parties.
  • Don’t take stakeholder management lightly. There are language and cultural differences between South Africa and Mozambique and you need to be equipped to operate in specific areas within the country – including differences between Maputo and Palma.
  • Be aware of the bureaucracy you will need to navigate. For example, authorisation to work must be submitted in the province where you are going to work and then sent on to Maputo. When registering a company, the documents also need to be translated into Portuguese, which adds time to the process – factor in the time required to get things done or find a local company to assist you.
  • Projects have allocated quotas for work permits which need to be sourced via the EPCs. A large percentage of the workforce must be local, so it is not easy to get a work permit, however, special project quotas can be negotiated and delegated to contractors and subcontractors. It will be important that you negotiate your quota of permits as a condition of your proposal.
  • Be aware of rules regarding your personnel on site, as well as their required rotations. Your staff need to be aware that the camps are closed with no entry and exit whilst working on site. You will also potentially need replacement personnel for rotations.
  • Understand contracting risk. International companies will want to de-risk their projects by sharing the risk with partners. Make sure you understand the project and contracting risks and can manage the risks you sign up for.

Despite these challenges, organisations that think differently and take a bold, collective approach will be well positioned to unpack these opportunities in a way that creates value for all.
